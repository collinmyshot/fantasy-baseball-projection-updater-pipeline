Building Fantasy-Forward Park Factors: A Residual-Based Park Model for Streaming Decisions
Generated: 2026-03-02 17:34 
Ugh, Another Park Factor Model?
Park factors (PFs) are something we all think about when evaluating both hitters and pitchers. Colorado and Cincinnati are feared starts for pitchers while being absolute havens for hitters - and for good reason! But once you move past the obvious parks at the extremes, PF math can feel opaque and sometimes disconnected from what we as fantasy players actually experience week to week. That is why I built this model.
Baseball Savant sets the standard for public park factors (https://baseballsavant.mlb.com/leaderboard/statcast-park-factors), but the table includes plenty of categories that are either less relevant for most fantasy formats (2B, 3B, xBACON) or less intuitive in day-to-day fantasy decisions (OBP, Hard Hit). FanGraphs park factors (https://www.fangraphs.com/tools/guts?type=pf) are also useful, but are less commonly referenced in fantasy circles and are built without fantasy directly in mind. Both models are trying to capture real-life value. Fantasy managers need something much more focused: how much a park changes BABIP-type hit risk (WHIP and start length) and how much it changes home-run blow-up risk.
So How Does This Model Work?
My methodology has three main pillars:
1. Actual - expected contact outcomes: if a park repeatedly over- or under-performs expected contact quality over a long sample, the park itself is likely the biggest driver of that difference.
2. Weather and baseball physics: this model controls for game-level weather when available (temperature, wind, humidity) and year-level drag effects, so we do not blame the park for what is really atmospheric or ball-level noise as the ball itself changes year-to-year.
3. Team defense: strong defenses can suppress observed BABIP independent of park geometry, so defense has to be controlled or park estimates get biased.
Concretely, this model incorporates every batted-ball event (BBE) from the start of the Statcast era in 2015 through the end of the 2025 regular season (2020 excluded because it was such a confounding year, both in real life and from a data perspective). That leaves 1,634,138 BBEs across 9 seasons. The primary residual is wOBAcon minus xwOBAcon, with component residuals for BACON and HR. Defense is included at the team-season level using an even blend of OAA, UZR, and DRS (OAA source: https://baseballsavant.mlb.com/leaderboard/outs_above_average; DRS/UZR source: https://www.fangraphs.com/leaders/major-league?stats=fld). I also split environmental effects into 1H (March-June) and 2H (July-September) to capture broad climatic seasonal shifts.
Nerd stuff alert: the model is hierarchical, with random effects for park-era, park-era-half, batter-season, pitcher-season, batting-team-season, and fielding-team-season. That is the core move that separates park context from roster quality. Invariance checks are reassuringly small: correlation between estimated park effect and home-team offense is -0.022, and correlation with home-team defense is 0.031. TL;DR: once talent and defense are controlled, repeatable venue-era residuals are treated as park signal.
Park Eras Matter (More Than You Think)
A big quality-of-life improvement in this build is explicit park-era splitting. Instead of pretending each team has one static park forever, we split eras around known moves, temporary homes, and dimension changes using data/manual/park_era_events.csv. This captures cases like Baltimore wall changes (2022 and 2025), Toronto temporary homes (Dunedin/Buffalo), Tampa Bay temporary move then return, Athletics move to Sutter Health Park, and the 2026 Kauffman dimension update. Era records include primary and secondary source URLs in-line. For 2026 outputs, we also maintain a verified home-park map for all 30 teams in data/manual/mlb_home_parks_2026_verified_sources.csv using MLB team pages (https://www.mlb.com/team) with secondary checks against current stadium listings. The full park-era event log table is provided in Appendix A.
How the Scores Are Built (Nerd stuff)
RMSE is the quick scorecard for "how wrong are we, on average?" Mathematically, I am calculating the square root of weighted mean squared prediction error for park-half residuals in rolling validation. We train on trailing windows and predict the next season to test forward stability, not just in-sample fit. Weighted holdout RMSE values: model=0.0211, zero-baseline=0.0231, prior-park-baseline=0.0169. Weighted correlation between predicted and realized holdout effects is 0.530 (prior-park baseline 0.557), with calibration slope 0.810.
For display, we normalize everything to a 100-centered index where 100 is league-average context. The final Overall PF then combines BACON, HR, and XBH with weights to emphasize the things we care most about in fantasy (0.45 BACON / 0.35 HR / 0.20 XBH), so the metric reflects both hit volume and damage quality.

	How It Compares to Savant and FanGraphs
It is important to sanity-check this model against models fantasy players already trust, so I validated directly against Savant and FanGraphs park outputs. Overall, there is nice correlative agreement between models, but the top-10 and bottom-10 of this fantasy-specific model overlap with Savant is 6/10 (of the top 10 parks) and 5/10 (of the bot 10 parks). While there is strong directional agreement with both FG and Savant models, the fantasy specific component does change the specific ranks. This is good news as it indicates that there may be value here instead of just using what Savant already provides.

	What This Uncovered for Streaming
The most useful takeaway is that "overall park factor" can hide very different pathways to run scoring. Coors grades as extremely BACON-heavy, while Cincinnati and Yankee Stadium tilt much more toward HR damage. Seattle and San Francisco remind us that suppressing contact quality and suppressing HR are not always the same thing. That matters for streaming: fly-ball pitchers and ground-ball/contact managers interact with these environments differently.
The 1H/2H split adds another practical layer by surfacing seasonal drift instead of pretending each park plays the same in April and August.

	Appendix B: Equations and Model Formulae
Event-level residual definitions:
* resid_i = wOBAcon_i - xwOBAcon_i
* bacon_resid_i = hit_on_contact_i - xBA_on_contact_i
* hr_resid_i = HR_on_contact_i - xHR_on_contact_i
* xbh_resid_i = XBH_on_contact_i - xXBH_on_contact_i
Primary mixed-effects model (conceptual fixed+random form):
* resid ~ half + temp + wind_speed + humidity + drag + defense_composite + (1 | park_era_id) + (1 | park_era_half_id) + (1 | batter_season_id) + (1 | pitcher_season_id) + (1 | fielding_team_season_id) + (1 | batting_team_season_id)
Component models (same random structure, outcome swapped):
* bacon_resid ~ same fixed/random structure
* hr_resid ~ same fixed/random structure
* xbh_resid ~ same fixed/random structure
Index scaling and aggregation:
* PF_component_idx = 100 * (1 + delta_component / baseline_component)
* Overall_PF_idx = 0.45 * BACON_idx + 0.35 * HR_idx + 0.20 * XBH_idx
* RMSE = sqrt(weighted_mean((realized - predicted)^2, n_bbe))
